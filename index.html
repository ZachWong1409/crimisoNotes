Lecture 1
IS Management in the Global Economy
IT vs IS · 3 Business Issues · Org Environment · Technology Trends · IS Mission
IT vs IS — core definitions
IT
The pervasive technology element that has revolutionized and restructured human work and society.
IS
Combines technologies, people, processes, and organisational mechanisms to foster IT use and improve organisational performance.
3 Business issues due to growth of IT/IS
1. Globalization
Worldwide expansion. Orgs must balance global IT/IS goals with local systems needs. Internet accelerates internationalization.
2. E-enablement
Leveraging IT/IS to build relationships with consumers and enterprises. Internet is the hub for business.
3. Business intelligence
Knowledge sharing and management. Transfer of knowledge between people. Most important asset = people and their knowledge.
Management of IT/IS — 3 shifts
Governance shift
Who makes IT decisions shifting from IS executives alone → collaborative effort between IS, the business, and constituencies.
Role shift
IT/IS role shifting from application delivery → system integration and infrastructure development.
Outsourcing
Development and management of relationships with external service providers is now a key IS management responsibility.
Organisational environment
External environment
Internet economy · Global marketplace · Business ecosystems · Faster business cycles (first-mover advantage) · Accountability & transparency · Rising societal risks (security, job losses)
Internal environment
Supply-push → demand-pull · Customer self-service · Real-time working · Team-based working (groupware) · Anytime/anyplace work · Outsourcing & alliances · Flatter organisations
Technology environment — 4 trends
Hardware
1950s–60s: Mainframe → 1970s: Minicomputer → 1980s–90s: PCs/client-server → Late 90s: Web/mobile → Now: AR, VR, AI Robots, IoT, Quantum computers.
Software
Early: TPS, in-house dev → Later: ERP, open systems, web-centric → Now: Collaboration, Social, AI/ML, Blockchain, Cybersecurity.
Data
1970s: DBMS → 1990s: Data warehousing & mining → 2000s: XML, web content → 2010s+: Big Data — structured, semi-structured, unstructured.
Communications
1980s: LAN/WAN → 1990s: Internet, TCP/IP, VoIP → 2010s+: IoT, Machine-to-Machine, Edge Computing, 4G/5G.
Key terms to know
IT vs ISGlobalizationE-enablementBusiness intelligenceExternal environmentInternal environmentTechnology environmentGroupwareDemand-pull vs supply-pushTPS → MIS → today
IT = the technology itself. IS = technology + people + processes + org mechanisms. Know 3+ examples from each environment side. Technology trends = direct review Q.
Lecture 2
Strategic Use of IT
Working Inward · Working Outward · Working Across · Loose/Close/Tight · Mass Customization
3 directions of strategic IT use ← backbone of Lec 2
Working Inward (B2E)
Using IT to improve internal operations and employee productivity. Key vehicle: intranet (benefits: 24/7 availability, lower maintenance, faster updates).
Working Outward (B2C)
Using IT to get closer to customers, enter new markets, enable mass customization. New experience curves and electronic tenders.
Working Across (B2B)
Using IT to collaborate with suppliers, partners, and co-suppliers. Build virtual enterprise / e-marketplace.
Evolution of strategic IT use
Mid 1980s
End-user computing — Working Inward (adoption of PCs and software).
Late 1980s
Transactional efficiency — Working Outward (gain competitive advantage).
1990s
Re-engineering — Working Inward (business process re-engineering / BPR).
Mid–late 1990s
Internet — integration into e-business models. Dotcom spiral began 1999.
Early 2000s
Back to basics — Working Across (leverage internet to work more closely with others).
2010 and beyond
All three simultaneously — IT at the forefront of business strategy.
Cheap & disruptive revolution + IT as a utility
Cheap revolution
CIOs shifting from expensive proprietary hardware → cheap generic products. Google runs on 100,000 cheap servers. Also: free open-source software, VoIP, free apps.
IT as a utility
IT becoming ubiquitous like electricity. Debate: does IT still give competitive advantage? Proponents say YES — creativity, segmentation, differentiation. IT systems are infinitely configurable.
Loose · Close · Tight relationships ← very examinable
Loose
Ad hoc, limited access to internal info. May or may not be confidential. Low risk & low cost.
Close
Two parties exchange info formally. Some info may be confidential. Moderate risk & cost.
Tight
Two parties share at least one business process. Highly confidential data. High risk & cost — requires full integration.
Working outward — key concepts
New experience curve
Using IT as basis of a product/service = moving to a new curve. More experience → connected curves of competitive advantage. Requires substantial investment.
Electronic tenders
The capability to monitor a product or service using computers. Examples: car diagnostics, package tracking. Main objective: get closer to the customer.
Mass customization
Long-tail phenomenon — shift from mainstream → niches. Internet enables personalization at scale. Examples: iTunes, YouTube, mobile applets.
Key terms to know
Working Inward/Outward/AcrossCheap & disruptive revolutionIT as a utilityNew experience curveElectronic tendersLoose/Close/TightIntranet (B2E)Mass customization / long tailCRMCo-suppliers
Loose/Close/Tight = risk increases with closeness. Electronic tenders = monitoring a product/service using computers. IT utility debate: IT like electricity, but creativity and management quality still differentiates.
Lecture 3
Managing IT Operations
IT Budget · Challenges · 5 Types of Outsourcing · Offshoring · Business Continuity
What are IT operations?
Day-to-day tasks related to managing technology infrastructure — applications, services, storage, networking, and connectivity. If not professionally run and backed up, a company could suffer a crash that shuts down business.
Typical IT budget breakdown
57% — Operations
The largest slice. Day-to-day running of IT infrastructure.
33% — Systems & programming
Of this 33%: 70% is maintenance, only 30% is new development.
10% — Admin & training
Overhead and staff development costs.
IT operational measures
External measures (what customers see)
System uptime / downtime · Response and turnaround time · Program failures
Internal measures (IS/IT people)
Computer usage as % of capacity · Disk storage used · Job queue length
Types of IT outsourcing ← direct review Q
Monolithic
Huge contracts covering almost entire IT operations outsourced to one vendor.
Transitional
Outsource legacy maintenance so staff can build new systems, OR outsource new dev and keep maintenance in-house.
Best-of-breed
Selective outsourcing by vendor specialty — desktop support, data centers, network mgmt, disaster recovery, cloud. No single vendor best in all areas.
BPO
Business Process Outsourcing — outsource all or most of a reengineered process (BPR) with a large IT component.
E-Business
Outsource development and operation of web sites. Lets companies move fast and stay flexible. Common in internet-based firms.
Managing IT outsourcing — 3 aspects
Organisational structure
Joint teams: Top-level (conflict resolution) · Operational (day-to-day) · Joint special purpose (pressing issues) · Committees (change management).
Governance
Laid out in a contract. SLAs define: responsibilities, performance metrics, penalties, and bonuses.
Day-to-day working
Manage expectations · Integrate staffs explicitly · Grant appropriate access · Hold joint social events · Communicate frequently.
Offshoring vs insourcing
Offshoring
Outsourcing to another country for lower labor costs. Challenges: cultural differences, communication, local laws. Must provide cultural training.
Insourcing
Delegating work to an internal but mostly independent sub-contractor. Parent-subsidiary model. Maintains tight control of contract job execution.
Business continuity — disaster recovery options
Hot site
Fully equipped, operational backup site. Ready to go immediately. Highest cost.
Warm site
Partially equipped site. Some setup required before use. Moderate cost.
Cold site
Space only — no equipment. Lowest cost, longest recovery time.
Internal resources
Multiple data centers · Distributed processing · Backup telecom facilities.
Key terms to know
IT operationsExternal measuresInternal measuresMonolithic outsourcingTransitional outsourcingBest-of-breed outsourcingBPOE-business outsourcingSLAOffshoringInsourcingHot / Warm / Cold site
57% of IT budget = operations. Hot site = fully ready, Cold site = space only. Know one-line definitions for all 5 outsourcing types.

IT Project Management
PMI 10 Areas · Triple Constraint · 3 Roles · 4-Phase Lifecycle · Why Projects Fail
Core definitions
Project
A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product or service. One-time initiative with a definite beginning and ending.
Project management
The application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to a broad range of activities to meet the requirements of a particular project.
IT project goal + triple constraint
Goal: Deliver a quality system that meets business needs, on schedule and on budget.
Scope ↔ Time ↔ Cost

A change in one factor automatically changes at least one of the others.

PMI — 10 knowledge areas
Project ScopeProject TimeProject CostHuman ResourcesQuality ManagementRisk ManagementCommunicationsProcurementProject IntegrationStakeholders
Project management roles ← direct review Q
Project Manager (PM)
Plans and executes the project. Needs technical knowledge + non-technical skills: ethical, conflict resolution, critical thinking, task-oriented, adaptable.
Project Sponsor
Business manager who owns and pays for the project. Fights for approval, ensures resources, provides funding. PM must maintain strong relationship with sponsor.
Project Champion
Business manager with high credibility who advocates for the project. Often a lower-level manager highly impacted by it. Enthusiasm + excellent communication.
Project lifecycle — 4 phases
1
Initiation — Deliverable: Project Charter (objectives, scope, assumptions, estimated benefits). Assess feasibility: economic, operational, technical, schedule, legal, political.
2
Planning — 3 components: Scheduling (WBS), Budgeting, Staffing. Deliverables: Statement of Work + Project Plan.
3
Execution & Control — Risk and uncertainty highest at start. Manage risks and conflicts. Deliverable: completed project.
4
Closing — Post-project review: What went right? What went wrong? What to do differently?
Phase 2 — 3 planning components ← direct review Q
1. Scheduling
Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) — subdivide work into tasks. Assign time estimates. Consider task dependencies and milestones.
2. Budgeting
Document anticipated costs. Common PM mistakes: too optimistic, leaving components out, inconsistent methodology.
3. Staffing
Right skill mix, proficiency, quantity, timing. May need CoE training, incentives, or outside contractors.
Why projects fail ← direct review Q
Poor specification
Tasks overlooked, sloppy requirements gathering.
Poor control
Failure to meet/control deadlines, delays in decision making.
Resource failure
Wrong estimates, insufficient or poorly managed resources.
Staffing problems
Key personnel leave, poor communication, lack of teamwork.
Poor planning
No quality/risk management, no contingency planning, poor costing.
Wrong project
Solved the wrong problem. Best PM can't salvage the wrong project.
Key terms to know
Project managementIT portfolio management10 PMI knowledge areasTriple constraintProject ManagerProject SponsorProject ChampionWBSScheduling / Budgeting / StaffingStatement of Work
PM plans/executes, Sponsor owns/funds, Champion advocates. Triple constraint: change one = change others. "Wrong project" is the sneakiest failure reason.
Lecture 5
Distributed Systems & Networking
5 Reasons · Centralized vs Distributed · 4 Attributes · Enterprise Types · Thin/Thick Client · Network Mgmt
5 reasons for networking
1. Share tech resources
Sharing expensive hardware/software stored on file servers across all network users.
2. Share data
Virtual teams share data; retrieve from other nodes; efficient B2B transactions.
3. Distributed processing
Information processing at multiple sites via telecom lines. Client/server splits workload.
4. Enhanced communications
Email, IM, Videoconferencing, Blogs, Social networks, strategic alliances.
5. Marketing outreach
Sharing data via Internet with consumers as a marketing and sales channel.
Centralized vs distributed
Centralized
All processing at one central computer. Terminals connect to it. Single unified database. Simple to manage but single point of failure.
Distributed
Processing spread over many computers (LAN or WAN). Independent components. Evolution: Mainframe → Client-server → Web → Internet.
4 attributes — what determines how "distributed" a system is? ← direct review Q
Where processing is done
Is workload spread to other machines? Requires system interoperability.
How devices are interconnected
Redundancy — at least one alternative route between nodes.
Where information is stored
Centralized vs distributed data storage locations.
Rules/standards + management
Security, operating procedures, how distributed units communicate.
Types of enterprise distributed systems
Host-based hierarchy
Central mainframe at top; terminals connect to it.
Decentralized stand-alone
"Islands of computing" — not truly distributed.
Peer-to-peer LAN
No hierarchy; all computers are equals on the network.
Hybrid enterprise-wide
Mainframe + departmental LANs connected via WANs. Most common today.
Client-server
Splits workload between client (UI/graphics) and server (databases/processing).
Internet-based
Extension of client-server via Internet; enables ubiquitous computing.
Web services (SaaS)
Programs request other web services to do tasks; pay only for needed functionality.
Thin client vs thick (fat) client
Thin client
Most processing on server. Client displays in browser. May have no hard drive. Needs constant server communication.
Thick/fat client
Bulk of processing on desktop PC. Less constant server comms — mainly for archival storage.
Network management — 4 types
Fault management
Identify, isolate, fix errors → document the fix. Uses SNMP notifications.
Configuration management
Monitor & maintain device configs as software and updates change.
Performance management
Track device/network performance; flag slow devices for updates.
Security management
Monitor network to maintain cybersecurity standards and compliance.
Key terms to know
Centralised systemsDistributed systemsClient-serverHost-based hierarchyDecentralized stand-alonePeer-to-peer LANHybrid enterprise-wideThin clientThick clientQoSIT architectureIT infrastructure
The 4 attributes question is a direct review Q. Thin = server processing. Thick = local processing. Distribute unless operations are interdependent (ERP) or culture doesn't support it.

Managing Telecommunication
Digital Convergence · Internet/Intranet/Extranet · 3 Attributes · 1G–5G · IoT · IS Dept 3 Roles
Definition
The flow of information among individuals, workgroups, departments, customer sites, regional offices, between enterprises, and with the outside world. Telecom shifted from voice → data. The Internet now underpins e-business.
Analog era vs internet (digital) era
Old (analog)
Circuit switching · Sine wave signals · Telephone, coaxial cable, fiber optics, PSTN, modems.
Internet era (digital)
Packet switching (intelligent routing) · Binary 0s & 1s · Handles voice (VoIP), email, multimedia, gaming. More robust.
Digital convergence
The merging of voice, data, and video into one digital form over IP. IP has become the protocol of choice. Affects 4 industries: IT, consumer electronics, telecommunications, and media. Example: Zoom, Skype (VoIP + video).
Internet · Intranet · Extranet
Internet
Worldwide PUBLIC network using TCP/IP.
Intranet
Private network INSIDE an organisation using TCP/IP — for employees only.
Extranet
Part of intranet extended to authorized external partners, suppliers, customers. Key for B2B e-commerce.
3 attributes that make the internet important ← direct review Q
Ubiquity
Global — any business has global reach with just a browser and website.
Reliability
Designed to survive crashes via alternate routing — other nodes accessible even if one fails.
Scalability
Has sustained incredible growth; well-designed sites can handle tens of millions of hits/day.
Mobile generations — 1G to 5G
1G
Analog signals · Circuit switching · Voice only.
2G
Digital signals · Circuit switching · Voice + SMS.
3G
Mobile WANs · Email, web browsing (BlackBerry, early iPhone).
4G
IP networks · Internet video · High-speed data.
5G
Up to 100× faster than 4G · Low latency · High reliability.
IS department — 3 roles in telecom ← direct review Q
1. Plan & create architecture
Set design rules & policies. Two principles: coherent interfaces (convergence) + transparency (network invisible to users). Ensure interoperability.
2. Manage telecommunications
Control costs — avoid fragmented procurement, underutilization, unauthorized use, billing errors. Use portfolio management.
3. Stay at the forefront
Monitor tech trends, explore new business models, stay aware of telecom regulations and policy changes.
IoT impact on telecommunication
Smart routing / navigationAsset trackingInteroperabilityIncreased bandwidth demandLow latency networksCloud storage & processingSecurity challengesPower consumptionGeographic coverage gaps
Key terms to know
Digital convergenceInternet / Intranet / ExtranetUbiquityReliabilityScalabilityMessaging (killer app)Wireless technologiesIoTPacket switchingBroadband1G–5G
3 attributes (Ubiquity, Reliability, Scalability) and IS dept's 3 roles are both direct review Qs. Extranet = intranet extended to external partners, NOT the public.

ALL business activities conducted electronically — marketing, HR, supply chain, logistics, customer service, accounting, billing, AND buying/selling.

C2C

G2C
Government to Citizens — e-government services.
M-Commerce
Via wireless/handheld devices — smartphones, tablets. Includes Location-Based Services (LBS).

Read-only, mainly text-based, no interactivity (early 1990s–2000s).
Web 2.0 — Social
Dynamic, interactive, user-generated content. Facebook, YouTube, Twitter.
Web 3.0 — Semantic
AI understands and interprets content. Personalized, machine-readable data.
Web 4.0 — Intelligent
Autonomous, higher intelligence. Uses blockchain, IoT, cloud computing.
Web 5.0 — Symbiotic
Fully integrated human-tech relationship. Emotional AI, AR/VR, brain-computer interfaces.

Track & analyze traffic and user behavior. Google Analytics: real-time, audience, acquisition, behavior, conversion.

Secure, transparent, tamper-proof transactions. Supply chain tracking. Used by Shopify, Microsoft, Expedia.
Big Data & AI
Personalization, fraud detection, supply chain optimization, predictive analytics, chatbots.
AR / VR
Immersive product visualization, virtual shopping, try-before-you-buy experiences.
IoT & 5G
Real-time inventory tracking, AR shopping experiences, edge computing for e-commerce.

IT/IS Planning
IT vs IS Planning · Strategic vs Operational · 5-Step Process · SWOT · Support vs Strategic Role
IT planning vs IS planning (short term)
IT planning
Focuses on technical infrastructure — hardware, software, networks, IT assets. Components: procurement, network planning, system integration, IT security, infrastructure management.
IS planning
Focuses on how IS supports business objectives. Goes beyond tech. Components: business analysis, system development, data management, user training & support.
Strategic IT planning vs strategic IS planning (long term, 3–5 years)
Strategic IT planning
Long-term roadmap for IT infrastructure. Key elements: business-IT alignment, technology assessment, IT roadmap, resource planning, risk management.
Strategic IS planning
Aligns IS/IT with long-term strategic objectives. Considers industry trends, risks, opportunities. Elements: business process analysis, major system selection, change management.
Benefits of information resources planning
Context for IT/IS decision-makingAlign IT/IS with business goalsBalance standardization vs agilityObtain capital investment approvalsAchieve integration and innovation
The 5-step multi-step planning process ← #1 most examinable
1
Assess current information resources — Inventory and evaluate tech and human resources. Compare to past plans, competitors, benchmarks (e.g. MTTR). Reconcile IS manager vs business manager views of the mission.
2
Create an information vision — A written expression of the desired future — how information will be used and managed. Represents senior management's long-term intent. Must align with future business strategy.
3
Design the IT architecture — The blueprint — depicts how IT resources will be deployed to deliver the vision. Specifies how tech and human assets will be used. Structural level, logical components, interrelationships.
4
Formulate the strategic IT/IS plan — Long-term objectives (3–5 years). 4 sub-steps: set objectives → external analysis → internal analysis → establish strategic initiatives. Uses SWOT analysis.
5
Formulate operational IT/IS plans — Short-term (up to 1 year). Details what must be done now. Specifies: expected benefits & risks, due dates, responsibilities, and precise expected results.
SWOT analysis (used in step 4)
S — Strengths
Internal positive factors the organisation can leverage.
W — Weaknesses
Internal limiting factors that need to be addressed.
O — Opportunities
External factors the organisation can capitalize on.
T — Threats
External risks that could undermine strategic initiatives.
Support role vs strategic role of IS
Support role
IS keeps the lights on — maintains infrastructure, processes transactions. Not central to competitive advantage.
Strategic role
IS enables business strategy and drives competitive advantage. Measured by: degree of org dependence on IS + degree IS enables current business strategy.
Key terms to know
IT planningIS planningStrategic IT planningStrategic IS planningInformation visionIT architectureSWOT analysisMulti-step planning processOperational IT/IS planSupport vs strategic roleBusiness-IT alignmentMTTR benchmark
Know all 5 steps by name AND description. Information vision = written expression of desired future. IT architecture = the blueprint. Strategic plan = 3–5 years; Operational = up to 1 year.

Cost & Benefits of IS
CBA · Hardware vs Software Costs · In-house/Buy/Outsource · Tangible/Intangible · TCO · Qualitative Assessment
What is cost-benefit analysis (CBA)?
A method to estimate the costs and benefits of decisions in order to find the most cost-effective alternative. Costs are mainly incurred now, while benefits come later. The longer the delay in receiving benefits, the greater the risk.
4 key elements of a CBA
1. Identifying costs
Hardware, software, development, implementation, training, maintenance and support fees.
2. Quantifying benefits
Identify and quantify both tangible and intangible benefits.
3. Timeframe
The period over which costs and benefits will be consumed or realized.
4. Monetary valuation
Assign monetary values for comparison. Costs are easier; benefits require estimation and projection.
Hardware vs software costs ← direct review Q
Hardware costs
Front-end (user interface, peripherals) · Networking equipment (routers, switches, encryption) · Back-end (servers, desktop PCs, maintenance)
Software costs
Development costs (in-house) or license/package costs (bought-in) · App dev tools · Security/encryption · Networking software · DBMS · Front-end packages
In-house vs buy vs outsource ← likely exam Q
In-house development
Develop internally. Advantages: better security, better control, better knowledge. Costs: training, capital, technical expertise, start-up costs.
Buy packaged software
Purchase off-the-shelf. Advantages: less financial risk, less technical risk, technology flexibility, focus on core business. Costs: specification costs, management overhead.
Outsource
Hire a firm to develop or modify. Advantages: lower personnel costs, tech flexibility, focus on core business, legal contract protection. Costs: specification costs, loss of added value.
Tangible benefits (directly quantifiable)
Direct cost savings
Automating processes replaces people; efficiency-based cost reductions; fewer staff = lower property costs.
Avoiding cost increases
IS can avoid costs such as transportation expenses.
Revenue increases
Increased sales through new services, delivery channels, or market penetration.
Staying in business
Sometimes introducing a new system is essential for the organisation to continue operating.
Intangible benefits (can be quantified to some degree)
Improved communicationsStaff moraleCustomer satisfactionReputationCustomer managementFlexibilityValue chain managementOrganisational learningDifferentiation
Total costs of ownership (TCO) ← direct review Q
TCO = a holistic view of costs over the entire lifetime of an investment — from acquisition to disposal. Goes beyond purchase price to include implementation, upgrades, maintenance, training, and admin costs.
Support

Disaster recovery
Duplication of facilities at alternate sites to ensure continuity.
Staff
Recruiting, training developers, maintainers, and users.
Maintenance
Bug fixes, enhancements, spare parts, skilled technicians.
Obsolescence
Does the product comply with industry standards? May become impossible to maintain.
Upgrades
Both hardware and software will likely need upgrading over time.

Systems quality
Reliability, features/functions, response time.
Information quality
Clarity, completeness, usefulness, accuracy.
Information use
Regularity of use, number of enquiries, duration, frequency of report requests.
User satisfaction
Overall satisfaction, enjoyment, software satisfaction.
Individual impact
Decision correctness, effectiveness, time to decide, productivity improvement.
Organisational impact
Achieving goals, cost-benefit ratio, ROI, service effectiveness.

IT Governance & IT Audit
Governance Hierarchy · 5 Objectives · IT Controls · IT vs IS Audit · 5-Stage Process · Tools
The governance hierarchy ← understand the chain
Corporate Governance Framework of rules & processes by which a company is directed and controlled. Sets overall standards.
IT/IS Governance Governs IT/IS within the org to align IT strategies with business objectives. Frameworks: ITIL, ISO/IEC 20000, COBIT, COSO.
IT/IS Control Mechanisms to manage, monitor, and mitigate risks associated with IT/IS.
IT/IS Audit Assessing and evaluating the effectiveness of IT/IS governance and control mechanisms.
Why corporate governance matters
Protection of stakeholder interestsEnhanced trust & credibilityRisk managementLong-term sustainabilityCompliance with laws & regulations
Real-world failures: Enron (2001) · HealthSouth (2003) · Satyam (2009) · Volkswagen (2015) · Wells Fargo (2016) — all due to weak governance.
IT governance — definition & frameworks
Processes, policies, and structures used to manage IT assets, resources, and operations in alignment with business goals. Involves managing and controlling key IT capability decisions to improve IT management, ensure compliance, and increase value from IT investments.
ITILISO/IEC 20000COBITCOSO
5 objectives of IT governance ← direct review Q
Ensures regulatory complianceGains competitive advantageSupports business goalsEncourages growth & innovationReduces risks
IT controls — types
Security controls
Firewalls, encryption, access controls, intrusion detection systems.
Access controls
Prevent unauthorized access; mitigate data breach risk.
Change management
Govern how changes to IT systems are planned, approved, tested, implemented.
Incident response
Minimize impact of incidents and enable quick recovery.
Data protection
Confidentiality, integrity, availability. Encryption, backup, classification, retention.
Compliance controls
Help avoid legal and regulatory issues.
Vendor management
Select, contract, and oversee third-party IT vendors and service providers.
Monitoring & reporting
Continuous assessment of IT systems and processes.
IT audit vs IS audit ← exam comparison Q
IT Audit
Scope: Broader — covers infrastructure, hardware, software, IT operations, AND information systems.

Objective: Assess overall effectiveness, efficiency, reliability of IT infrastructure.

Controls: General IT controls — access, change management, backup procedures.

Compliance: Broader regulatory frameworks (financial regulations).
IS Audit
Scope: Narrower — evaluates controls, risks, governance of information systems specifically. May include non-IT aspects (people, processes, data).

Objective: Focus on data confidentiality, integrity, availability, security posture.

Controls: User access controls, authentication, encryption, data classification.

Compliance: Information security standards.
In practice, many orgs use these terms interchangeably — the distinction can be indistinct.
IT audit process — 5 stages ← direct review Q
1
Planning & scoping — Define what needs to be audited and why. Identify potential risks and objectives.
2
Assessment — Evaluate IT controls and security measures. Test selected controls and sample data. Identify weaknesses and deficiencies.
3
Reporting — Document findings and recommendations in the audit report.
4
Communication & action — Share findings with stakeholders. Discuss concerns. Monitor implementation of corrective actions.
5
Closure & improvement — Formally close the audit when objectives are met. Reflect on lessons learned and update audit process for continuous improvement.
IT audit software tools ← direct review Q
Audit management systems
Centralized platform for planning, executing, managing audits. Features: risk assessment, workpaper management, evidence collection, issue tracking, reporting.
Data analysis tools
Analyze large volumes of data to identify patterns, anomalies, and potential risks.
Vulnerability assessment tools
Scan network, servers, apps, endpoints to detect security vulnerabilities.
Network monitoring tools
Capture traffic, monitor devices, provide insights on utilization, anomalies, security incidents.
Configuration management tools
Review and manage configuration settings of systems, apps, and devices.
